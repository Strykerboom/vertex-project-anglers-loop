Inshore: What You'll Need

For inshore fishing, you'll need light to medium rods, spinning reels, and a variety of baits such as shrimp or small baitfish. A tackle box with hooks, sinkers, and leaders is essential.

Inshore: Rod and Reel Setups

Use a 6-7 foot medium-action rod paired with a spinning reel spooled with 10-20 lb braided line. This setup is versatile for most inshore species.

Inshore: Baits

Live shrimp, mud minnows, and small crabs are excellent baits for inshore fishing. Artificial baits like soft plastics and topwater lures also work well.

Inshore: Artificial Lures

Soft plastics, paddle tails, and jerk baits are effective for inshore species like redfish, trout, and flounder. Use natural colors in clear water and brighter colors in murky water.

Inshore: How to Find Spots

Look for areas with structure such as docks, oyster beds, and grass flats. Tidal creeks and mangroves are also productive inshore fishing spots.

Inshore: Rigs

Use a Carolina rig or a popping cork rig for live bait. For artificial lures, a jig head is a versatile option.

Inshore: Rigging Your Gear

Attach a fluorocarbon leader to your main line to prevent fish from seeing the line. Use a loop knot for better lure action.

Inshore: Tackle

Carry a variety of hooks, jig heads, and sinkers. A small tackle box with essentials like pliers, scissors, and extra line is key

Inshore: How to Cast

Practice accurate casting to place your bait or lure near structure. Use a sidearm cast for low-hanging areas like docks.

Inshore: How to Catch Fish Consistently

Fish during tidal changes, use the right bait for the target species, and pay attention to water temperature and clarity.

Inshore: How to Have Fun

Bring friends or family, enjoy the scenery, and take breaks to appreciate the experience.

Inshore: How to Stay Safe

Wear a life jacket, stay hydrated, and be aware of weather conditions. Avoid wading in areas with strong currents.

Surf: What You'll Need

For surf fishing, you'll need a long rod (9-12 feet), a spinning reel with a high line capacity, and a sand spike to hold your rod. Waders or waterproof boots are helpful for accessing deeper areas.

Surf: Rod and Reel Setups

Use a 10-12 foot surf rod paired with a spinning reel spooled with 20-30 lb braided line. This setup allows for long-distance casting and handling larger fish.

Surf: Baits

Fresh cut bait, live sand fleas, and shrimp are excellent choices for surf fishing. Clams and squid are also effective for attracting a variety of species.

Surf: Artificial Lures

Metal spoons, poppers, and soft plastics are effective for surf fishing. Use lures that mimic baitfish to attract predatory species like bluefish and striped bass.

Surf: How to Find Spots

Look for areas with breaking waves, sandbars, and troughs. Fish tend to gather in these areas to feed. Early morning and late evening are the best times to fish.

Surf: Rigs

Use a fish finder rig or a double-drop rig for surf fishing. These rigs keep your bait in the strike zone and are effective for catching multiple species.

Surf: Rigging Your Gear

Attach a pyramid sinker to your rig to keep it anchored in the surf. Use circle hooks to improve hook-up rates and reduce harm to the fish.

Surf: Tackle

Carry a variety of hooks, sinkers, and swivels. A tackle box with pliers, scissors, and extra line is essential. Bring a bait bucket to keep live bait fresh.

Surf: How to Catch Fish Consistently

Fish during tidal changes and use fresh bait. Pay attention to water temperature and wave patterns to locate feeding fish.

Surf: How to Stay Safe

Be aware of rip currents and avoid wading too deep. Wear sunscreen and stay hydrated to protect yourself from the sun.

Offshore: What You'll Need

For offshore fishing, you'll need heavy-duty rods, conventional reels, and terminal tackle like circle hooks and swivels. A boat with safety equipment, including life jackets and a first aid kit, is essential.

Offshore: Rod and Reel Setups

Use a 6-7 foot heavy-action rod paired with a conventional reel spooled with 80-100 lb braided line. This setup is ideal for targeting large species like tuna and marlin.

Offshore: Baits

Live bait such as mackerel, sardines, and squid are highly effective for offshore fishing. Cut bait and trolling lures are also commonly used.

Offshore: Artificial Lures

Trolling lures, poppers, and jigs are effective for offshore fishing. Use brightly colored lures to attract pelagic species like mahi-mahi and wahoo.

Offshore: How to Find Spots

Look for areas with underwater structures like reefs, wrecks, and drop-offs. Use a fish finder to locate schools of fish and baitfish activity.

Offshore: Rigs

Use a trolling rig or a bottom fishing rig depending on your target species. Heavy-duty leaders and circle hooks are recommended for large fish.

Offshore: Rigging Your Gear

Use crimping tools to secure heavy leaders and swivels. Ensure your knots are strong and reliable to handle the pressure of large fish.

Offshore: Tackle

Carry a variety of hooks, leaders, and sinkers. A gaff and a fish bag are essential for landing and storing large fish.

Offshore: How to Fish Sustainably

Follow catch limits and avoid targeting endangered species. Use circle hooks to reduce bycatch and release unwanted fish carefully.

Offshore: How to Catch Fish Consistently

Monitor water temperature, currents, and baitfish activity. Use a fish finder to locate schools of fish and adjust your techniques accordingly.

Offshore: How to Stay Safe

Wear a life jacket, monitor weather conditions, and ensure your boat is equipped with safety gear. Always let someone know your fishing plans.

Offshore: How to Handle the Fish Properly

Use a gaff or net to land large fish. Handle them carefully to avoid injury to yourself and the fish. Release unwanted fish quickly and safely.
